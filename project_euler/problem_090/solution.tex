\section{Problem Statement}
Two cubes each have 6 faces with distinct digits from $\{0, \ldots, 9\}$. By placing two cubes side-by-side, all two-digit perfect squares (01, 04, 09, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81) must be displayable. The digits 6 and 9 are interchangeable. How many distinct arrangements of two cubes permit this?

\section{Solution}

\subsection{Enumeration Space}
Each cube is a 6-element subset of $\{0, 1, \ldots, 9\}$. The number of possible cubes is:
\[
\binom{10}{6} = 210
\]

Since the two cubes are unordered (swapping gives the same arrangement), the number of pairs is:
\[
\binom{210}{2} + 210 = \frac{210 \times 211}{2} = 22{,}155
\]

\subsection{Validity Check}
For each pair $(C_1, C_2)$, define $C_i' = C_i \cup \{9\}$ if $6 \in C_i$, and $C_i' = C_i \cup \{6\}$ if $9 \in C_i$ (the 6/9 equivalence).

A pair is valid if for each required pair $(a, b)$:
\[
(a \in C_1' \wedge b \in C_2') \vee (a \in C_2' \wedge b \in C_1')
\]

The required pairs are:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccccccccc}
(0,1) & (0,4) & (0,9) & (1,6) & (2,5) & (3,6) & (4,9) & (6,4) & (8,1)
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Note that (4,9) and (6,4) together with the 6/9 rule require careful handling: 64 needs a 6 on one cube and 4 on the other, which with the interchangeability means a 6-or-9 on one and 4 on the other.

\subsection{Result}
Exhaustive enumeration yields:
\[
\boxed{1217}
\]
valid arrangements.

\section{Answer}

\[
\boxed{1217}
\]
