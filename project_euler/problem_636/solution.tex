\section{Problem Statement}

Define $F(n)$ as the number of ways $n$ can be written as a product of one natural number, two squares, three cubes, and four fourth powers, with all base numbers distinct. Find $F(1{,}000{,}000!) \bmod 1{,}000{,}000{,}007$.

\section{Mathematical Analysis}

\subsection{Representation}

We seek the number of representations:
\[
n = a \cdot b_1^2 \cdot b_2^2 \cdot c_1^3 \cdot c_2^3 \cdot c_3^3 \cdot d_1^4 \cdot d_2^4 \cdot d_3^4 \cdot d_4^4
\]
where all bases $\{a, b_1, b_2, c_1, c_2, c_3, d_1, d_2, d_3, d_4\}$ are distinct positive integers.

\subsection{Prime Exponent Distribution}

For $n = N!$, by Legendre's formula:
\[
v_p(N!) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \left\lfloor \frac{N}{p^i} \right\rfloor
\]

For each prime $p$, we must distribute $v_p(N!)$ as:
\[
v_p(N!) = \alpha_p + 2(\beta_{1,p} + \beta_{2,p}) + 3(\gamma_{1,p} + \gamma_{2,p} + \gamma_{3,p}) + 4(\delta_{1,p} + \delta_{2,p} + \delta_{3,p} + \delta_{4,p})
\]

\subsection{Generating Function}

The generating function for the exponent distribution at each prime is:
\[
G(x) = \frac{1}{1-x} \cdot \frac{1}{(1-x^2)^2} \cdot \frac{1}{(1-x^3)^3} \cdot \frac{1}{(1-x^4)^4}
\]

The coefficient $[x^e]G(x)$ gives the number of ways to distribute exponent $e$ without the distinctness constraint.

\subsection{Distinctness via Inclusion-Exclusion}

The distinctness of bases is enforced through inclusion-exclusion on the base assignments. Since each base is a product of prime powers, and the prime exponents at different primes are distributed independently, we can use a multiplicative approach.
